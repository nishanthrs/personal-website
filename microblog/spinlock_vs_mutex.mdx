After diving into some codebases today, I started looking into locks and how code was synchronized.
I never realized how many freaking locks there are in the folly C++ library, but there are two main ones
I needed to understand: spinlocks and shared mutexes.

Let's say a thread is currently executing a critical section and is locked behind a spinlock: `folly::SpinLock`.
If another thread hits the critical section and is blocked by the spinlock, it'll continue *spinning* or burning
CPU cycles until it's unlocked. However, if we use a shared mutex: `folly::SharedMutex`, the blocked thread will
start spinning very briefly, then yield preemptively to the scheduler (`futex`) to start executing another thread,
and wait until mutex is unlocked.

Spinlocks usually don't work well in multithreaded services (> 1 thread per core) because they hog the CPU for
useless work and sacrifice throughput. Spinlocks also suck under high contention because they thrash the CPU cache.

At first, it seems like spinlocks are great for extremely short critical sections since putting a mutex to sleep and waking it up again is wasted overhead.

However, it's possible that thread A gets preempted in the middle of executing its critical section, leaving the blocked
threads spinning endlessly until thread A resumes again. In the worst scenarios, thread A may not even resume again since
the blocked threads are hogging the CPU. This can drive up spinlock tail latencies a lot.
On top of that, since `folly::SharedMutex` specifically operates like a *hybrid* mutex and spins initially, this overhead is mostly avoided anyways.
It seems like the recommendation is to never use spinlocks in user-space code.
